What the Evidence Says

The framework: design, implementation, toolkit

4 Design
  • Start from local realities, not a generic template
  • Challenge harmful gender norms directly
  • Build skills step by step, not all at once
  • Address multiple causes of violence together
2 Implementation
  • Train and support strong facilitators
  • Follow the program but adapt it to the culture
4 Toolkit
  • Session plans with clear goals
  • Safe-space rules and trauma awareness
  • Tools to track who shows up and what changes
  • Referral plans for when someone needs help
Ask: who caught something similar? Walk through each column briefly. Emphasize that content alone is not enough -- delivery and tools matter equally. Spend no more than 2 minutes here.
Why This Matters for You

Three things to remember when you design anything

Good content fails without strong facilitation. The person running it matters as much as the curriculum.

One workshop changes nothing. Norm change requires repetition, practice, and social reinforcement over time.

Design for transfer. If participants leave with slogans instead of actions, you wasted their time.

Click the Listen button to have the three takeaways read aloud in a British accent. Then ask the room: which of these three is hardest to get right? Take 2-3 responses. Then say: now let me show you a curriculum that tries to do all three.

The Man Box Module

Four sessions, from naming norms to taking action

  • 1 Define the Man Box — surface local masculine expectations. Name what's inside and outside the box.
  • 2 Costs of rigidity — analyze how rigid masculinity harms self and relationships.
  • 3 Power and violence — connect norms of control to violence using real-life scenarios.
  • 4 Alternatives and action — practice ally behaviors and make concrete commitments.
Facilitation Moves
  • Set group agreements early and enforce respectful participation
  • Use scenario-based discussion before personal disclosure
  • Validate emotion while redirecting harmful statements to reflection
  • Close each session with a concrete, low-risk behavior practice
Walk through the four sessions emphasizing the arc: name it, feel it, connect it to violence, then act. Then point to the facilitation panel: these four moves are reusable in any difficult conversation. Ask: which one is hardest to do?
Your Turn

Design a 1-hour peer workshop for UA students

Use what you just learned. This is a skill, not a quiz — you will submit your design when we're done.

  • 3 min Choose your foundation. Pick one THRIVE module and one evidence principle from Video 1. Write a one-sentence goal for your workshop.

  • 2 min Pair up. Pitch your workshop to a partner in 60 seconds. They give you one concrete challenge.

The Twist

You are not submitting anything.

That workshop you just designed? It's yours. The facilitation move you planned? You own it. The challenge your partner gave you? That's the kind of feedback that makes real programs better.

You just practiced the exact skill this entire lecture was about: designing prevention that transfers.

Keep your design. Refine it. Use it.
